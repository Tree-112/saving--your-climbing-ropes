Annotated Bibliography

Source 1

Nikonov, A., Nagode, M., Klemenc, J., & Emri, I. (2026). Effect of weight on dynamic ropes' responses in indoor climbing. Proceedings of the Institution of Mechanical Engineers, Part P: Journal of Sports Engineering and Technology, 240(2), 428–435.

This study examined how climber body weight affects the mechanical response of dynamic climbing ropes during a fall, testing 25 rope samples across five weight groups (23–104 kg) with repeated drop tests. Maximum impact force increased with both climber weight and repeated use, while deceleration showed a non-monotonic relationship with weight, meaning lighter climbers don't always experience lower risk. The study concludes that current safety standards, calibrated for an 80 kg climber, may not adequately protect climbers outside that range, and recommends that gyms track climber weight to determine when to retire a rope. This source establishes the safety problem at the center of this project: rope performance is not uniform across users, which raises the stakes for understanding how ropes degrade over time and use.

Source 2

Zhao, Z., Hu, Z., He, G., Liu, Q., Li, H., Xie, D., Ning, X., & Ning, F. (2025). Impact failure mechanism of kernmantle ropes for fall protection from heights. Textile Research Journal, 95(11–12), 1287–1298.

This study investigated the physical mechanisms behind kernmantle rope failure under repeated fall impacts, combining force-displacement testing, infrared thermal imaging, and scanning electron microscopy on nylon rope samples. The researchers identified three interacting failure mechanisms: shear failure from uneven stress distribution in the braided structure, abrasive failure from internal core strands rotating against each other, and thermal failure from heat accumulating inside the rope core, which caused fiber-to-fiber adhesion and reduced impact resistance. This source explains the mechanism behind rope degradation at a structural level, providing the physical basis for why ropes weaken with repeated use.

Source 3

Sedláček, D., Roso, M., Viel, L., Perotto, N., Caven, B., Hasler, M., & Nachbauer, W. (2024). Ageing of climbing ropes with and without hydrophobic coating. Proceedings of the Institution of Mechanical Engineers, Part P: Journal of Sports Engineering and Technology, 238(1), 44–54.

This study tested whether a hydrophobic (Teflon) coating changes how climbing ropes age under four months of outdoor weather exposure, comparing coated and uncoated ropes through UIAA drop tests, tensile testing, and spectroscopy. While impact force on the climber stayed consistent across both rope types, the uncoated rope's falls-to-failure dropped by 50% over the test period compared to a 20% decline in the coated rope, suggesting the coating meaningfully slows fatigue from weathering, though it also accelerated degradation of the outer sheath yarns specifically. This source anchors the popular science article and proposes a potential mitigation strategy, showing that coating choice measurably affects how well a rope holds up outdoors.
